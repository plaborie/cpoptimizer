Review.* Detailed review. (Required)

The paper studies the complexity of different variants of a scheduling problem on multiple machines with machine incompatibility between jobs being represented as an incompatibility graph: there is an arc between two jobs if and only if the two jobs cannot be executed on the same machine. 

The results presented in the paper extend some recent studies on machine incompatibility graph that were focusing on makespan minimization while the present paper is on the minimization of the total completion time. The present results are significant, they consist on polynomiality results on some restricted versions of the problem (problems with identical machines or with machine-independent processing time), some hardness results, and some fixed-parameter tractability results (in particular depending on the number of machines, the largest processing time or the number of job types).

While the paper is dense, it is clearly written and organized, with a general presentation of the problem and notations, a summary of the results (Table 1) and different sections for the different types of results (polynomiality results, harness results, FPT) with some illustrative examples when needed.

There is something that is not clear: in the introduction you say "More formally, an incompatibility graph G = (J;E) is part of the input, and an edge (j,j') in E signifies that in a feasible schedule j and j' cannot be assigned to the same machine. In this paper, we study variants of R||sum (w_j)C_j in which the incompatibility graph is a collection of cliques corresponding to the groups of jobs mentioned above". So it seems that the paper studies the case of disjoint cliques, that is, a restricted case of graph G. Do you confirm ? The introduction of the incompatibility graph G may suggest that you handle the case of a general graph.

Some more detailed comments:

Questions
---------

Q1: Do you confirm that the paper does not study the case of a general incompatibility graph G but the restricted case where G is a union of disjoint cliques ? Do you have an idea of the impact of taking into account general graphs in the study ?

Q2: Many results in the paper rely on the asumption that finding the optimal sequencing of jobs on a machine is not required or that it is "easy" to find one. In many real-life problems, this is not the case, for instance if there are release dates, the sequencing problem on a machine is NP Hard (1| r_j |sum C_j). Do you have an idea how the results presented in the paper could extend to these more complex cases ? Both from a theoretical point of view (FPT on additional parameter sets) and also from the perspective of efficiently solving the problems ..
